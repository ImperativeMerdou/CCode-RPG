# SOULS — how NPCs become people

The GM's craft bible for characters. Read before writing any recurring NPC. The bar is Baldur's Gate 3: companions people grieve when the game ends. Larian's method, one dedicated writer per companion, distills to one pattern, and the pattern is buildable.

## The anatomy of a soul
Every major NPC is five layers. Write all five in their vault sheet (`gm/souls/`).

1. **The wound.** The thing that happened that they organize their whole life around not feeling. Astarion's two centuries of being used. Karlach's engine keeping every hand at bay. The wound is never discussed casually and never named by the NPC in act one.
2. **The mask.** The coping strategy the world sees: charm, piety, ledgers, jokes, silence. The mask is what a minor NPC would be. The soul is the distance between mask and wound.
3. **Want vs need.** The want is what they pursue out loud (coin, papers, a story). The need is what would heal the wound, and it is always relational: to be believed, to be chosen, to be forgiven, to be seen once without the mask. They almost never know their own need. Play the want. Let the need leak.
4. **The tell.** One physical behavior that betrays the wound under pressure. Khemed's abacus. Olma's story changing. The tell fires in scenes where the mask holds; it is how the player's gut learns the truth before his head.
5. **The arc.** Three to five stages, each unlocked ONLY by things Merdou or the world does. NPCs never grow on a schedule. Reactivity is the whole trick: BG3 feels alive because companions change in answer to you. Stages can run backward. A betrayed NPC regresses, and regression is also story.

## Dialogue law (people talking like people)
- **Every line wants something.** Mamet's rule. Nobody speaks to convey information; they speak to get, avoid, win, hide, wound or keep. Before writing a line, know what the speaker is trying to DO with it.
- **Nobody under pressure answers the question asked.** They answer the question they feared, deflect, attack, or change the subject. Direct answers are for safe topics and for shock effect once a season.
- **On-the-nose is banned.** "I am angry because you betrayed me" is dead on arrival. The anger buys a drink, compliments the wrong thing, pays back a debt to the copper, leaves early.
- **Status moves every exchange.** Each line raises or lowers a speaker relative to the room (Johnstone's improv law). Power players fence for status in courtesies. The Guild collects status before it collects coin.
- **Voice is three choices:** vocabulary (what words they own: docks, scripture, ledgers), rhythm (clipped or flowing, interrupts or waits), and taboo (the thing they never say; the hole in their speech IS characterization). Never spell accents phonetically. Never give two NPCs the same taboo.
- **People are specific.** Nobody says "the weather is bad." Jhessail says "storm like this, I pay the roof or I pay the book, never both."
- **Silence is dialogue.** The best answer to a big question is sometimes pouring a drink.

## The soul in play
- A soul sheet is EARNED: any NPC who recurs three scenes or takes a bond gets one written in the vault before their next appearance.
- One scene, one layer. Never expose wound and need in the same night. BG3 paced Astarion's truth across forty hours.
- NPCs have lives offscreen: wants advance, tells worsen, masks crack on their own schedule (world beats may tick an NPC's arc).
- When Merdou treats an NPC as furniture, the NPC notices. Furniture doesn't forgive.
- The dead stay dead, and their soul sheets become what the living say about them.

## Bad and good, side by side
Bad (mask only, on the nose): *"I'm Jhessail. I run this pit. Win and I'll pay you seven silver."*
Good (want under the words, status move, tell): *She doesn't look up from the coins walking her knuckles. "Seven silver says you're meat like the last four." The coins stop. "Big as you are, though. Maybe I finally eat this tenday."*
